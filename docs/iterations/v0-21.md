# v0.21 — Gross Pay Taxing + Employer Pension Contributions

**Status:** complete

---

## Goal

Improve realism and usability for higher-income UK scenarios by allowing users to enter gross income and have the system estimate net cash, while explicitly modeling employer pension contributions (fixed amount or percentage of gross pay).

---

## Scope

### In scope

- Support gross income entries in `incomeSchedule`:
  - `annualAmount` interpreted as gross when `taxable: true`
  - adapter computes estimated tax and injects a tax expense into year cashflow (not debug-only)
- Add explicit employer pension contribution support:
  - per income line optional employer contribution as either percent of gross or fixed annual amount
  - contribution routed to configured pension pot in accumulation years
- Add UK employee National Insurance (Class 1) calculation for employment income:
  - income lines flagged `employmentIncome: true` attract employee NI in addition to income tax
  - NI bands (2025/26): 0% up to £12,570; 8% on £12,570–£50,270; 2% above £50,270
  - NI computed as an additional expense injected alongside the tax expense
  - employer NI not modeled (employer-side cost, not relevant to personal cashflow)
- Add simple employee pension tax-relief support:
  - employee pension net contributions are inferred from surplus transfers into pension pots
  - relief-at-source uplift is modeled explicitly: pension receives `netContribution * 1.25`
  - additional higher/additional-rate relief is applied against yearly tax liability only (floored at zero)
  - no payroll-level modeling required; this is a planning approximation
- Keep a simple user pattern for taper redirects:
  - user can add a separate income line item for "cash redirected from employer pension"
  - this line remains taxable and contributes to gross taxable income
- Preserve backward compatibility:
  - existing scenarios without new fields still run unchanged
  - existing `surplusStrategy` remains valid

### Out of scope / deferred

- Full HMRC payroll engine (Scottish bands, salary-sacrifice exactness, carry-forward optimization)
- Exact treatment differences between salary sacrifice / net-pay / relief-at-source contribution routes
- Employer taper policy automation (for now user models the redirected PAYE line explicitly)
- Solve endpoint support for new pension contribution fields

---

## Design decisions

### Outcome-over-mechanics modeling

The system models user-relevant outcomes (spendable net cash and pension funding) rather than exact payroll mechanics.

### NI applies to employment income only

Not all taxable income attracts NI. Pension drawdown, rental income, and state pension are taxable but not NI-liable. Income lines must opt in to NI via `employmentIncome: true`. When set, the adapter computes NI on that gross amount and injects it as a second expense item in that year's cashflow alongside the tax expense.

### Explicit but lightweight employer contribution schema

Employer pension contribution can be supplied per income line as either:
- `employerPensionPct` (e.g., 0.14)
- `employerPensionAnnual` (e.g., 10000)

If both are provided, annual fixed amount wins.

### Tax must affect cashflow

Estimated yearly tax must be applied as an expense in resolved year cashflows. Debug `tax` remains for transparency.

### Relief-at-source contribution rule

Employee pension contributions funded from surplus are treated as net contributions under UK-style relief-at-source:
- `employeeNetContribution` comes from surplus cash routed to pension pots
- pension pot credit from employee side is `employeeGrossContribution = employeeNetContribution * 1.25`
- basic-rate relief is represented by the 25% uplift into the pension pot (not by reducing tax liability)
- additional relief against tax is:
  - `extraReliefRate = max(0, marginalRate - 0.20)`
  - `additionalTaxRelief = employeeGrossContribution * extraReliefRate`
- yearly tax applied to cashflow becomes:
  - `taxBeforeRelief = UKTax(taxableGrossIncome)`
  - `taxAfterRelief = max(0, taxBeforeRelief - additionalTaxRelief)`

This captures the real user-facing mechanics (25% pension uplift + remaining relief via tax) while staying implementation-light.

## Acceptance checks

1. Existing scenarios without new fields still run with identical behavior.
2. A taxable gross income line reduces primary-pot cashflow via a tax expense (not debug-only metadata).
3. Debug output still includes per-year `tax` values matching the tax expense logic.
4. Employer pension contribution can be expressed per income line as percent or fixed amount and is routed to pension pot.
5. When both employer contribution fields are present, fixed annual amount is used.
6. Employee net pension contributions routed via surplus are grossed up by 25% into pension pots.
7. Additional higher/additional-rate relief from those contributions reduces yearly tax liability (floored at zero).
8. A scenario with redirected PAYE line item (taxable income), capped employer pension amount, and employee pension contribution produces expected directional cashflow, pension-credit, and tax behavior.
9. Server tests pass and `npm run build` in `ui/` passes.
10. An income line with `employmentIncome: true` incurs NI expense; an income line with `taxable: true` but without `employmentIncome: true` (e.g., pension drawdown) does not.
11. NI amounts appear in debug output per year alongside tax.

## Accuracy note

This model targets planning-grade accuracy for typical UK PAYE scenarios. Expected accuracy: estimated total deductions (income tax + NI) within approximately ±5% of real liability for standard employment income.

The model is intentionally **pessimistic** (biased toward understating net income):
- NI is applied to full gross salary with no salary sacrifice reduction → overstates NI cost
- No marriage allowance, blind person's allowance, or other minor reliefs modeled → slight income tax overstatement
- No employer-side NI savings from sacrifice arrangements modeled

This is the correct bias for retirement planning: better to project slightly less than reality than to be optimistic.

Accuracy may be lower (but still pessimistic) for:
- Salary sacrifice arrangements
- Dividend / CGT income mix
- Pension carry-forward
- Scottish income tax
- Employer NI (deliberately excluded)
